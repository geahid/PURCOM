Quiz 1 Communication Foundations

Part 1 – What is Communication?

Communication (Two Definitions)Definition▼
  • Involves verbal and non-verbal expression of thoughts and feelings. A yawn or a phone call exemplifies non-verbal and verbal messages, respectively.
  • Is sharing ideas through a medium, focusing on interaction.
Communication ComponentsKey Concept▼
ComponentDescription
SourceThe sender crafts the message, such as an author or speaker.
MessageThe shared meaning, like poems or road signs.
ChannelThe medium, like a phone or letter.
ReceiverThe recipient who listens or reads carefully.
FeedbackEssential for understanding, expressed in various forms like nods.
EnvironmentThe physical and emotional setting of communication.
ContextInvolves the expectations of the sender and the receiver and the common or shared understanding through the environmental signals.
InterferenceAlso known as barrier or block that prevents effective communication to take place.
Kinds of InterferenceTypes▼
TypeDescription
PsychologicalBarriers are thoughts that hamper the message to be interpreted correctly by the receiver.
PhysicalBarriers include competing stimulus, weather and climate, health and ignorance of the medium.
Linguistic and CulturalBarriers pertain to the language and its cultural environment. Words may mean another in different cultures.
MechanicalBarriers are those raised by the channels employed for inter-personal, group or mass communication. These include cellphones, laptops and other gadgets used in communication.

Part 2 – Effective Communication

Michael Osborn (2009) — Nine Principles of Effective CommunicationKey Concept▼
  • Clarity – makes speeches understandable. Fuzzy language is absolutely forbidden, as are jargons, cliché expressions, euphemisms and doublespeak language.
  • Concreteness – reduces misunderstandings. Messages must be supported by facts such as research data, statistics or figures. To achieve concreteness, abstract words must be avoided.
  • Courtesy – builds goodwill. It involves being polite in terms of approach and manner of addressing an individual.
  • Correctness – glaring mistakes in grammar obscures the meaning of a sentence. Also, the misuse of language can damage your credibility.
  • Consideration – messages must be geared towards the audience. The sender must consider the recipient's profession, level of education, race, ethnicity, hobbies, interests, passions, advocacies and age when drafting or delivering a message.
  • Creativity – in communication means having the ability to craft interesting messages in terms of sentence structure and word choice.
  • Conciseness – simplicity and directness help you to be concise. Avoid using lengthy expressions and words that may confuse the recipient.
  • Cultural Sensitivity – today, with the increasing emphasis on empowering diverse cultures, lifestyles, and races and the pursuit for gender equality, cultural sensitivity becomes an important standard for effective communication.
  • Captivating – you must strive to make messages interesting to command more attention and better responses.

Part 3 – Ethics & Globalization

Ethics in CommunicationDefinition▼

Ethics is a branch of philosophy that focuses on issues of right and wrong in human affairs.

Ethical Communicators:

  • Respect audience
  • Consider the result of communication
  • Value truth
  • Use information correctly
  • Do not falsify information
GlobalizationDefinition▼

Globalization is the communication and assimilation among individuals, ethnicities, races, institutions, governments of various nations supported by technology and compelled by international trade.

It is not a new process or concept. Years before the advent of technology, people had been purchasing and selling each other properties, goods and other objects of certain value.

Possible Cultural Barriers:

  • Cultural relativism
  • Lack of knowledge of others' culture
  • Discrimination and harassment
  • Language differences
Krizan (2014) — Strategies to Become an Effective Global CommunicatorKey Concept▼
  • Review communication principles
  • Analyze the message receiver
  • Be open to and accepting of other cultures
  • Learn about cultures and apply what is learned
  • Consider language needs

Part 6 – Media Literacy & Text

Key Concepts of Media LiteracyKey Concept▼

The Key Concepts of Media Literacy framework serves as a basis for developing a critical understanding of the content of mass media and can be very helpful in the construction of media texts for different purposes.

TextDefinition▼

The term text includes any form of written, spoken or media work conveying meaning to an audience. Text may use words, graphics, sounds and images in presenting information. It may also be in oral, print, visual or electronic form.

Multimedia PresentationsKey Concept▼

Characteristics: Visually oriented; use different modalities.

Steps in Making an Effective Multimedia Presentation:

  • Know the purpose of the presentation
  • Know the audience
  • Gather information
  • Use a variety of resources
  • Do not forget to cite resources
  • Organize the information
  • Check Technical Issue
  • Be Creative

Technology enhances different forms of communication such as: intrapersonal, interpersonal, small group, public, and mass communication.

Pecha Kucha – a 20-slide PowerPoint presentation which moves automatically to each succeeding slide every after 20 seconds. Very helpful to keep track of the time.

Part 7 – Blogging & Informative/Persuasive Communication

BloggingDefinition▼

Blogging has become one of the effective ways of communicating and networking among students, professionals, businessmen, public officials, etc.

Blog is short for weblog. Blogs are personal journal websites on which a user can type an entry and add images, videos and links to other websites.

According to Richardson (2006), the use of blogs has been an engaging and effective way to promote writing skills of primary students, particularly when student peers provide feedback to the blog.

Informative CommunicationDefinition▼

Informative Communication involves giving than asking. As an informative communicator, you want your receivers to pay attention and understand, but not to change their behavior.

Factors (Osborn, 2009):

  • We seek to expand our awareness of the world around us.
  • We seek to become more competent.
  • We have an abiding curiosity about how things work and how they are made.
Persuasive & Argumentative CommunicationTypes▼

Persuasive Communication is an art of gaining fair and favorable considerations from our point of view.

Argumentative Communication relies heavily on soundproof and reasoning. The nature of proof has been studied since the Golden Age of Greece and has been improved through time.

Lucas (2007) — To avoid defective argumentation, avoid:

  • Defective evidence: Misuse of facts, Statistical fallacies, Defective Testimony, Inappropriate evidence
  • Defective Patterns of reasoning: Evidential fallacies, Slippery slope, Confusing facts with opinions, Red herring, Myth of the mean, Flawed proofs, Defective arguments

Part 8 – Public Speaking & Making Inquiries

Public Speaking & Speech Delivery MethodsKey Concept▼

Public Speaking is a process of speaking in a structured, deliberate manner to inform, influence or entertain the audience.

Speech is the term to refer to the body spoken expression of information and ideas.

MethodDescription
Reading from ManuscriptAppropriate when the speech is long and when details are complicated and essential such as they need to be given completely.
MemorizedSpeech requires a speaker to commit everything to memory; excellent for short messages although also used for long pieces in oratorical, declamation and other literally contest.
Extemporaneous SpeakingMay have a long or short preparation. The Speaker may use an outline to guide him through his speech to achieve better organization and to avoid leaving out details.
ImpromptuMeans at the spur of the moments; since there is very minimal or no time for preparation.
Making InquiriesKey Concept▼

Making inquiries is the act of asking questions to get information. It is an important communication skill used in school, business, and everyday life.

Types of Inquiry:

  • Personal Inquiry – asking information personally.
  • Business Inquiry – asking information related to business or services.
  • Academic Inquiry – asking questions related to school or education.

Ways of Making Inquiries: Letter, Email, Phone Call, Face-to-Face Conversation

Important Parts of an Inquiry: Greeting, Introduction, Purpose, Questions, Closing

Importance of Making Inquiries:

  • It helps people get accurate information.
  • It improves communication skills.
  • It helps with decision making.
  • It avoids misunderstanding.
Communication for Work PurposesKey Concept▼

Communication for work purposes is the professional way of sharing information to get a job done correctly. It is not just casual talking; it is about being accurate, organized, and ethical in your specific field.
